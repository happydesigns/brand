Color decisions start with the job of the color, not the shade number. Most screens should be built from Warm White, Graphite, and Sand first; Coral, Peach, Petrol, and the state colors enter only when their role is needed.

The shade tables below are implementation reference. Pick the color family by role first, then choose a shade for the required contrast, surface, or interaction state.

## Core pair

Warm White and Graphite form the stable contrast pair. Every other color is layered on top of this foundation.

| Color | Token | Hex | Role |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| <span class="hd-color-chip" style="--chip: #FAF7F2"></span> | `warmWhite` | `#FAF7F2` | Page background, primary surface |
| <span class="hd-color-chip" style="--chip: #242423"></span> | `graphite` | `#242423` | Default text, premium fills, dark base |

## Usage rules

- Use `warmWhite` and `graphite` as the stable contrast pair. Do not substitute other colors for this role.
- Use `coral` sparingly — one or two accents per view. Overuse erodes its value as the signature color.
- Use `sand` for all visible structure: borders, separators, grid gaps, and subdued panels.
- Use semantic utilities (`bg-default`, `text-body`, `border-default`) in components, not raw shade classes. Raw shades require a paired `dark:` variant; semantic utilities handle both modes automatically.
- Use lighter shades (50–200) for backgrounds and tinted areas, mid shades (400–600) for text and icons on light surfaces, dark shades (700–950) for text and fills on dark surfaces.
- Do not fill large surfaces with saturated accent colors. Reserve mid-to-dark accent shades for interactive elements, not layout backgrounds.
- Dark mode shifts semantic tokens one step lighter (e.g. `coral-500` → `coral-400`) to maintain appropriate contrast on dark surfaces.
